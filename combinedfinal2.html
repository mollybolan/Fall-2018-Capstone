41,000 Syracuse residents were below the poverty line in 2016. Syracuse's 2016 poverty rate was 13th worst in the United States, and the city is currently ranked first for highest concentration of black and latino poverty. WAER set out to report on the different factions of poverty in the Salt City. City Limits is the resulting audio project that tells the stories of Syracuse residents and the organizations that have been established to help them.

A History of Poverty in Syracuse

Redlining Begins

The US goverment aims to increase home ownership by providing federally-backed mortgage loans to citizens they determined were most likely to maintain their payments. Those living in predominantly African American neighborhoods are often denied these loans.

1937

The 15th Ward Thrives

The 15th Ward, an ethnically diverse but redlined neighborhood, is an oasis for residents of color in a city rampant with discrimination. Eight of every nine African Americans live in the 15th Ward.

1950
1956

Federal Highway Act is Passed

The US government aims to connect cities across America by building a network of highways. Highways are only built in yellow and redlined areas.

1957

Construction of I81 Begins

The 15th Ward is razed as construction of the new highway begins. Over 27 city blocks are demolished and residents are forced to relocated with little compensation. Many move to the city's south side.

White Flight & Property Blight

Between 1970 and 2010, Syracuse's white population drops by 50% as those who can afford to move to the suburbs. They leave vacant homes in their wake, left to fall into disrepair, thus decreasing the values of properties inhabited by those still living in the city.

2010

The Future of I81

I81 has outlived its useful life. The city is in the process of determining whether to eliminate the viaduct in favor of a community grid. Some argue that the highways as is is vital to the city's economy and should not be changed. A decision likely won't be made until 2019.

2017+

The city of Syracuse is now determining the fate of Interstate 81 which is known for being a racial and economic viaduct that slices through the city.
